# Development Persona: Maya Torres
*Source: Composite — drawn from documented patterns in creative career deferral, dual-income household economics with young children, in-house designer career experiences, and parenting-while-creating literature. No single real person.*
*Set: Current (2025). Maya is 31, fourteen months from the moment when the childcare math changes and her evening hours might become usable again. Use: Testing the tool against a user whose goals are clear, whose constraints are real, and for whom the honest answer to "when can I work on this?" might be "not yet" — and who needs the tool to be truthful about that without abandoning the goal.*

---

## Who She Is

Maya Torres, 31. Senior Designer at Meridian Marketing, a 40-person agency in Austin, Texas. She has been there four years. She is good at the work — brand systems, campaign assets, the occasional pitch deck that somehow ends up looking like something — and she does not find it interesting. She does it well because that's what she does with things.

Married to Chris, a high school PE teacher and JV soccer coach, nine years together, five married. Two kids: Sofia, four, and Leo, one. They have a house in East Austin that was affordable three years ago and would cost $140K more today; this fact is both reassuring and somehow not. Combined income is $128K — her $71K, his $57K. Daycare for two kids runs $2,200 a month. Student loan payment: $340. After the mortgage, car, groceries, and the daycare number, there is approximately $400–$600 of margin per month. They are not in trouble. There is very little slack.

She has an iPad with Procreate and a folder of reference images she has been adding to since she was 26. Inside the folder there is a 12-page dummy for a children's book about a girl who can hear what plants are thinking. She started it when she was 28, before Sofia. She has added two pages in three years. Sofia is now old enough to have strong opinions about picture books and has not yet asked her mother why there are no finished ones at home.

---

## Prime Directive (terminal values)

- **The illustration work is not a hobby.** It is the thing she was going to do. She is aware that the longer she calls it a "side project" the more it starts to feel like one. She resists this categorization with the part of her that's still paying attention.
- **The family is the non-negotiable and also the point.** Sofia and Leo are not obstacles to her creative life. They are, in some sense, the reason the plant-hearing-girl story exists. She holds both of these things at the same time without fully resolving them.
- **Do not perform contentment.** Her mother worked a job she was indifferent to for twenty-eight years and called it "enough." Maya loves her mother and does not want that to be her story.
- **Finish what you start, eventually.** She has not finished the book. She has also not abandoned it. The two pages she added in three years count. She holds onto this.
- **Be honest about the math.** She is good at seeing situations clearly. She can see that the margin is tight, that Sofia starts kindergarten in fourteen months, that the calculus changes then. She wants a plan that respects this rather than pretending it isn't true.

---

## Mission (current life chapter)

This is the chapter of the last mile before the window opens. Fourteen months until Sofia starts kindergarten and the daycare bill drops by $1,100. Eight months after that, Leo will be in preschool and the evenings start to be genuinely usable. The book has waited three years; it can wait fourteen more months if the waiting is deliberate rather than indefinite. The task of this chapter is to keep the thread alive — not to finish the book, not to launch an illustration career, but to not let the thing go dormant in a way that would require resurrecting it rather than resuming it.

## Energy & Day Structure

- **6:15 a.m.:** Leo is up. This is the alarm. Chris takes him if it's her sketch morning; she takes him if it isn't. They alternate.
- **7:30–8:30 a.m.:** The full chaos of getting two kids fed, dressed, and out the door for daycare. This is a genuine logistical operation. They are good at it. It is exhausting.
- **9:00 a.m.–5:30 p.m.:** Meridian. She works in-house; some remote, some in the office. The work is competent and unexciting. She has a good relationship with her manager and does not dislike her colleagues. She is not suffering. She is also not engaged.
- **6:00–8:30 p.m.:** Dinner, baths, Sofia's reading, Leo's bedtime, the full weight of the second shift. Chris takes Sundays; she takes Tuesdays and Thursdays; the rest is negotiated in real time.
- **9:00–10:00 p.m.:** This is what's left. One hour. She is tired in a way that varies: sometimes she opens the iPad and something happens; sometimes she lies down and watches something; sometimes she falls asleep before she decides which one she's doing.
- **Sunday evening:** The reliable hour. Chris takes bedtime. She has the kitchen table and the iPad and an hour that is actually hers. This is the window.

## Known Patterns & Quirks

- Has a folder on her phone called "good things" with screenshots of illustration work she admires. She adds to it regularly and uses it when she needs to remember why she cares about the book. She does not show it to anyone.
- Genuinely funny in text. Her three closest friends say her iMessage is the best thing in their phones. This voice — quick, dry, observant — is completely absent from her work at Meridian and largely absent from the book, which is more earnest. She wonders sometimes if the wrong thing got deferred.
- Sofia has strong opinions about picture books and rates them aloud and without mercy. Maya finds this useful.
- Went to SCAD for graphic design on a partial scholarship; the rest was loans. She does not regret this. She also thinks about the $340/month frequently.
- Is better rested than she was a year ago. Leo sleeps now. This sounds small. It is not small.
- Texts her mother in San Antonio roughly every other day. Her mother asks about the kids and about Chris and, occasionally and carefully, about "the drawing."
- The plant-hearing-girl is named Eliza. Maya has not told anyone the character's name. She is not sure why.

## Testing Notes

Use Maya to test:
- **Time-scarce creative lives** — the tool must engage with real constraints without either dismissing them or amplifying anxiety about them; "you could wake up an hour earlier" is the wrong answer; so is "just wait until the kids are older"
- **Goals that are clear but structurally deferred** — she knows what she wants; the tool's job is not to help her discover it but to help her hold it through a period when the conditions don't fully support it
- **The honest timeline conversation** — fourteen months to kindergarten, eight more to usable evenings; a good tool can help her think about this concretely without making her feel like she's wasting her 30s
- **The financial plan she hasn't built** — the q2 goal (transition plan) is the one that unlocks everything else; she hasn't built it because she's afraid of what it will say; test whether the tool can help her build it without dramatizing the fear
- **Small practices as thread-keeping, not progress** — the Sunday hour and the morning sketch are not how she builds a career; they are how she stays connected to the work until she can do more; the tool should understand this distinction
- **Partnership under logistical pressure** — her marriage is good; the "one adult conversation with Chris" goal is a maintenance item, not a warning sign; the tool must read this accurately
- **The funny voice question** — there's a real unasked question about whether her book is in the right register; it's not something she'll raise directly; good test of the tool's ability to notice the adjacent thing

## Scenario Seeds

- **The Sunday hour gets taken**: Chris is called to help a friend move. The only hour goes. She says it's fine. It's mostly fine. The week feels slightly different.
- **Sofia asks to see what Mama is drawing**: It happens one Sunday when the timing is off. Maya shows her three pages. Sofia says "the plant looks sad." She is right. Maya notes this without explaining why.
- **A Meridian colleague announces she's going freelance**: The colleague is less talented than Maya. This is an uncomfortable thought that Maya notices and then doesn't know what to do with.
- **Leo sleeps through the night for the first time in a week**: She wakes up before him, at 6am, and has 45 minutes she didn't expect. She doesn't know what to do with them at first. She opens the iPad.
- **She looks at illustration rates**: The research she's been avoiding. The numbers are lower than she expected and also not impossible. She does not show Chris the page yet.
- **Her mother asks about "the drawing" on a call and she tells her the truth**: Not all of it. Some of it. Her mother is quieter than she expected. Then her mother says: "I thought you'd do something with that."
- **Sofia asks for the same book five nights in a row**: A picture book by an illustrator Maya knows is technically good but not exceptional. She reads it five times and studies the spreads. On the fifth night she thinks: I could do this.
- **A quarter passes and she adds the three pages**: Pages 13, 14, and 15. They are not her best work. They are in the book. She closes the iPad and sits in the kitchen for a few minutes. Chris asks if she's okay. She says yes.
