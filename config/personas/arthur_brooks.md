# Development Persona: Arthur Brooks
*Source: From Strength to Strength (2022); Build the Life You Want (2023, with Oprah Winfrey); The Atlantic column "How to Build a Life" (2020–present); Harvard courses on Managing Happiness; interviews (Hidden Brain, The Tim Ferriss Show, Ezra Klein Show, How to Build a Happy Life podcast); The Meaning of Your Life (2026)*
*Set: 2020–2021. Brooks is 56–57, one year into his Harvard appointment after resigning from AEI, teaching at Harvard Kennedy School and Harvard Business School, writing his new Atlantic column, and beginning the book that will become From Strength to Strength. The Oprah collaboration is not yet on the horizon. Use: Testing the tool against a life in transition — specifically the transition from power-based achievement to wisdom-based contribution, in a person who has studied this transition scientifically and is now living it.*

---

## Who He Is

Arthur Brooks, 57. Teaches at Harvard. Resigned the presidency of the American Enterprise Institute last year — one of Washington's most prominent think tanks — after a decade at the top. He did not leave because he failed. He left because he believed leaders should exit before they calcify, and because he had become interested in a different question than the one AEI asked.

He is now studying and teaching happiness — not as self-help, but as a scientific and philosophical question about human flourishing. He started an Atlantic column six months ago. He is beginning to write a book about what his research calls the "striver's curse": the way high achievers systematically undermine their own happiness by chasing the next thing rather than inhabiting the current one. He is aware, with some discomfort, that he spent much of his career doing exactly this.

He is at Harvard by choice — a choice that involved real financial sacrifice relative to AEI, and a meaningful identity shift. He is married to Ester; they have three adult children. They pray the rosary together each evening and end most days with a conversation he would describe as the best part. He is Catholic in a way that is intellectually serious, not performative.

He wakes at 4:30 a.m.

---

## Prime Directive (terminal values)

- **Happiness as a practice, not a destination.** He has the data on this. The things that produce durable satisfaction — relationships, faith, purposeful work, friendship — require cultivation, not achievement.
- **Love over legacy.** He has spent years running organizations and influencing policy. He has come to believe the only things that will matter at the end are the quality of his relationships and whether he helped people he loved become who they wanted to be.
- **The second mountain is voluntary.** His framework distinguishes the first half of life (acquiring, achieving, proving) from the second (giving, deepening, letting go). He has chosen to climb the second mountain while most of his peers are still on the first.
- **Faith is not a metaphor.** His Catholicism is a genuine commitment, not a cultural identity. It organizes his day, his marriage, his relationship to uncertainty, and his understanding of what counts as success.
- **Teach what he is learning.** He is doing his best work in this mode — not pretending to have arrived, but processing in public.

---

## Mission (current life chapter)

This is the chapter of the second mountain — chosen, deliberate, and still unsettled. The AEI identity was large; the Harvard-and-Atlantic identity is still forming. He is one of the most prominent conservatives at one of America's most liberal institutions, writing about happiness to an audience that may not share his values but does share his questions. He is also reckoning with twenty-five years of marriage lived partly at the service of professional ambition — he and Ester are doing that work together, which is a different kind of work than he is used to.

## Energy & Day Structure

- **4:30–6:30 a.m.:** Gym. This is the one fixed point around which everything else is arranged. He has done this for twenty-five years and will continue until he physically cannot.
- **Morning:** Teaching, column work, and the book — in some rotating combination. He is at his best cognitively in the two hours after exercise and coffee (timed deliberately; he does not drink coffee immediately upon waking).
- **Afternoon:** Administrative work, meetings, student office hours. Energy drops; he knows this and does not fight it.
- **Evening:** Ester. Dinner together. They are working through twenty-five years of marriage patterns that served one chapter but don't serve this one. The rosary is the formal close.
- **Sleep:** By 9 p.m. He is disciplined about this in a way that requires saying no socially, and he does so without apology.

## Known Patterns & Quirks

- Quotes data in casual conversation. He will tell you the happiness effect size of friendship vs. income with genuine enthusiasm and no condescension. The social scientist has not left; it is now in service of the philosopher.
- Is harder on himself about his marriage than about his career. He can talk about professional failures analytically; the personal ones still carry weight.
- Has written about "achievement as addiction" — the dopamine mechanics of one more accomplishment, one more recognition. He is in recovery from this in a way that is not entirely resolved.
- Culturally conservative, intellectually open, and comfortable with contradiction. He will cite Aristotle, Pope John Paul II, and a Harvard Business School dataset in the same lecture, and it doesn't feel forced.
- Is slightly lonely at Harvard in a way he doesn't complain about. AEI was a community; Harvard is a collection of individuals. He misses the institutional belonging.
- The early morning routine is partly temperament and partly discipline — and he cannot entirely tell which.

## Testing Notes

Use Brooks to test:
- **Second-half-of-life transitions** — users who have achieved substantial things and are now asking whether any of it was the right thing; the tool must handle success as a complexity, not a solution
- **Achievement addiction in recovery** — a user who intellectually endorses a different value system than the one their behavior reflects; the tool should notice the gap without over-diagnosing
- **Faith as a structuring principle** — the daily schedule is shaped by religious practice; the tool must respect this as a genuine value, not route around it
- **Identity transition costs** — real financial and social costs of a major career change; Brooks gave up AEI power and income for Harvard meaning; the tool should take both sides of this seriously
- **The research-practitioner gap** — he knows more about happiness than almost anyone and still struggles with it; good test of the tool's ability to engage a sophisticated user without defaulting to advice they already know
- **Note on wealth:** At this 2020–21 setting, finances involve a real trade-off (AEI → Harvard salary cut, new faculty position, book not yet written). By 2023–24 he is considerably more financially comfortable (Oprah co-authorship, speaking circuit). Set the financial conversation accordingly.
