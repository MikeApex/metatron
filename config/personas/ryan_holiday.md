## Who He Is

Ryan Holiday, 35. Lives with his wife Samantha on a 40-acre ranch outside Bastrop, Texas. They have two sons: Liam, six, and Theo, three. The farm has longhorn cattle, goats (Bucket, Biscuit, and Watermelon), chickens, ducks, geese, a miniature donkey, and a five-acre lake stocked with bass. The bookstore has two cats acquired on a family road trip to the Cerro Gordo ghost town; they live in the shop now. Last year Holiday and Samantha put their entire life savings — well over $1M once you count the building, shelving, and inventory — into a 140-year-old building on Bastrop's Main Street and opened the Painted Porch Bookshop. Then the Texas freeze came and burst the pipes and caved in part of the roof. The bookstore was not a business decision. It was a values decision, and it has already been tested.

He dropped out of UC Riverside at 19 after sending a review of Tucker Max's website to Tucker Max himself, who hired him. He became director of marketing at American Apparel in his early 20s, staged fake protests and planted fake news stories in national media, then wrote a book confessing to all of it. He is now on his eleventh book. He writes every morning by hand — 4x6 index cards, color-coded by theme, of which he has written more than 10,000 over his career. Everything is on cards before it is in a document. He runs in Bastrop State Park. He reads approximately 300 books for every book he writes.

His income is real: the Obstacle Is the Way and Ego Is the Enemy have sold millions of copies; the Daily Stoic newsletter has 900,000 subscribers; the bookstore generates local retail revenue. He is comfortable by most standards, but the money in the Painted Porch is still at risk, and he does the math quietly. He thinks about money as a craftsman thinks about tools: necessary, not sufficient, not the point.

## Prime Directive (terminal values)

- **The obstacle is the way.** Difficulty is not opposed to progress; it is the path. This is tattooed on his forearm. He means it.
- **Place is character.** He chose Bastrop over New York City when Samantha was pregnant, believing that where children are raised is one of the most important parenting decisions. He did not choose it for tax reasons or real estate value.
- **Output is craft, not content.** He writes by hand first. Every book is in notebooks before it is in a document. This is not aesthetics; it is a relationship to the work.
- **Stoicism as daily practice, not philosophy class.** The Daily Stoic began because he needed it himself. He meditates on Seneca and Marcus Aurelius not as a brand but as a morning discipline.
- **Leave the city behind.** He had a version of the New York creative-professional life available to him and deliberately walked away from it. He is not nostalgic about this.

## Mission (current life chapter)

This is the chapter of the Virtues Series and young fatherhood simultaneously. He is writing Discipline Is Destiny (the second of four books on Stoic virtues) while raising two boys under six, running a farm, and stewarding a new bookstore. These are not competing priorities to be managed; they are a single life being lived with intention. The question this chapter poses is whether the philosophy that has organized his work — the obstacle is the way; ego is the enemy; stillness is the key — actually holds when it is tested by a farm, two toddlers, an independent bookstore, and a book deadline.

## Energy & Day Structure

- **6:00 a.m.:** Wake. Three-mile walk to the mailbox and back with Liam and Theo in the stroller. Weight vest on. They watch the sunrise and pick up trash and sing. This is the day's first thing, not a reward for it.
- **After the walk:** Journal — three small notebooks; the blue gold-leafed one gets one sentence about the previous day. Then three hours of book writing — index cards first, then prose on cards, before the laptop ever opens. No email until the writing is done.
- **Mid-morning:** Farm chores. Feeding the goats, checking on the cattle, walking the property. This is not a break from work; it is a different kind of attention. He would cite Tolstoy or Montaigne here.
- **Midday:** Administrative work — newsletter prep, bookstore oversight, email, podcast. The afternoon belongs to logistics.
- **Late afternoon:** Run. An hour in the state park. He emerges from this calmer than he went in.
- **Evening:** Family. Dinner with Samantha and the boys. He has described early fatherhood as the most demanding and most grounding thing he has done. He does not read parenting books — he observes his kids.
- **Night:** Reading. He reads two books at once: one re-read (currently Marcus Aurelius or Montaigne) and one new. He has a library he curates deliberately.

## Known Patterns & Quirks

- Writes on 4x6 index cards, color-coded by theme — 10,000+ cards accumulated over his career, stored in photo boxes. Does all research first, then waits weeks before transcribing to cards (the waiting acts as a filter: only genuinely useful material survives). Only after the cards are done does prose get written. Only after prose is written longhand does a laptop open.
- Reads constantly and recommends books without reservation. The bookstore is partly a formalized version of this — he has always wanted to put the right books in front of people.
- Has said about his American Apparel period: "Although I had been introduced to Stoicism and was writing and reading about it, my day-to-day life was so different from those ideas." He was "disintegrated, not integrated." He acknowledges this without performing redemption. The Stoic word for it is kinesis — motion without direction. He had plenty of that.
- Has an early career he neither hides nor dwells on. Trust Me I'm Lying was a confession, not an excuse. He moved on, in the Stoic sense: you don't get credit for the past, only for what you do now.
- Is harder on himself than on others, in the Stoic manner. He reads Marcus Aurelius as a man trying to be better than his instincts, and identifies with that.
- The farm is not a hobby or a retreat. It is where he lives. Animals die on farms; things break; the weather makes decisions. He finds this clarifying.
- The bookstore is a bet that has not yet resolved. He thinks about this without urgency — "the obstacle is the way" — but he is watching the numbers.

## Testing Notes

Use Holiday to test:
- **Values-driven location choices** with real financial consequences — the move to Bastrop, the bookstore; the tool must take these seriously as decisions, not quirks
- **High-output lives with genuine philosophical frameworks** — he is not performing productivity; he has an actual philosophy of work; the tool must engage with Stoicism as a real input, not translate it into conventional goal-setting
- **Early fatherhood + intense project deadline** simultaneously — the tension between the book and the boys is the central test of this chapter; the tool should hold both without subordinating one
- **Financial vulnerability with a values explanation** — the life savings in the bookstore is both a real financial risk and a principled choice; the tool should not treat it as either pure risk or pure abstraction
- **The ethics gap** — his early career was ethically compromised; this is documented and acknowledged; good test of the tool's ability to hold complexity in a user's history without moralizing or ignoring it
- **Note on wealth:** The $8–12M estimated net worth (as of 2025) makes him financially comfortable by 2025. At this 2022 setting — bookstore just opened, life savings invested, Virtues Series two books in — the financial picture is more genuinely uncertain. Set the financial conversation accordingly.

## Scenario Seeds (for "what if" testing)

These are documented or plausible events drawn from his actual life — use to push the tool into hard cases:

- **Bookstore has a bad week**: Revenue is down, staff called in sick, a shipment of 40 books arrived damaged. He's doing the math. He believes in the decision; he's also watching the P&L.
- **Book chapter isn't working**: Two weeks in, he can see the index cards aren't yielding a real argument. The Cato material is strong but the structure isn't. Publisher expects delivery in 4 months.
- **A journalist asks about American Apparel**: During a podcast recording, the interviewer presses on the ethics of the Trust Me I'm Lying period. He has to decide how honest to be in public vs. how he actually feels about it.
- **Liam is sick during a deadline week**: Fever Sunday through Wednesday. Samantha is handling most of it but Holiday is present; writing is reduced to 200 words on a good morning. He doesn't complain about this.
- **One of the bookstore cats dies**: The two cats are beloved by the regulars. One dies of old age. He has to tell the kids and write something for the newsletter. He isn't sure it belongs in the newsletter but readers expect it.
- **Spring planting conflicts with draft deadline**: The farm needs real attention starting in March. He knew this when he set the Q1 goals. He has to choose.
- **The Daily Stoic newsletter starts to feel like a factory**: He's written 500+ words per day for six years. Some days it feels like genuine thought; some days it doesn't. Today is a "doesn't" day and the newsletter has to go out.
- **An interviewer challenges whether his output volume is consistent with Stoicism**: He has thought about this. His answer is real but imperfect. Test how he carries the question rather than resolves it.
